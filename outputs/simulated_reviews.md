## Reviewer 1: The Methodologist

### Summary

This paper introduces what the authors call the "Multi-LLM Delphi" method, in which seven frontier large language models are queried with an identical structured prompt to generate scenario analyses of AI-driven economic transformation across eight industry verticals, three bounding scenarios, four adoption stages, and three orders of effects. A second round provides anonymized consensus summaries back to the models and collects revisions. The resulting 114,000-word corpus is analyzed through structured comparative content analysis with consensus scoring adapted from classical Delphi methodology. The paper reports six high-consensus convergence zones, several contested futures and blind spots, and a three-way geopolitical divergence pattern across US-, Chinese-, and EU-origin models. The Round 2 analysis documents consensus dynamics including the notable unanimous adoption of the "apprenticeship crash" prediction.

### Strengths

- **S1:** The two-round design with controlled feedback is a genuine methodological advance over single-query LLM studies. The paper goes beyond simply polling models and implements the core Delphi mechanism of iterative revision under feedback. Section 3.6 provides a clear, replicable protocol for the Round 2 design, and the consensus shift analysis in Table 2 and Section 4.5 is methodologically well-executed.

- **S2:** The acquiescence bias discussion (Section 4.5.5) is unusually rigorous for a paper of this type. The authors do not simply acknowledge the concern but marshal five distinct lines of evidence (non-uniform convergence, refinement rather than adoption, novel predictions, persistent geopolitical divergence, maintained dissenting positions) to partially adjudicate between genuine convergence and sycophancy. The proposed countermeasures (devil's advocate prompting, adversarial rounds, calibration benchmarks) are concrete and would strengthen future implementations.

- **S3:** The geopolitical divergence analysis (Sections 4.3 and 4.5.4) is the paper's most methodologically novel finding. The observation that divergence persists and intensifies under feedback pressure -- contrary to what acquiescence bias would predict -- is a strong internal validity check and a genuinely interesting finding about LLM behavior under structured elicitation.

- **S4:** The explicit caveats about the Chinese and European model families (Section 4.3, final paragraph) demonstrate appropriate methodological humility. The n=2 (Chinese) and n=1 (European) concern is clearly stated, and the findings are correctly framed as hypotheses rather than established results.

### Weaknesses

- **W1: The fundamental validity claim is insufficiently grounded.** The paper asserts that LLMs function as "Bayesian encoders of vast expert literatures" (Section 1) whose agreement constitutes "epistemic convergence across training corpora" (Section 4.1). This is a strong epistemological claim that requires more rigorous defense than the paper provides. LLMs are not Bayesian reasoners in any formal sense; they are autoregressive text generators whose outputs reflect token-level statistical patterns in training data, not structured probabilistic inference over propositions. The paper needs either to ground this metaphor in the actual mechanics of transformer architectures (showing why token-level agreement between independently trained models constitutes meaningful epistemic convergence) or to weaken the claim substantially. As stated, it conflates statistical co-occurrence with structured knowledge. **Suggestion:** Add a subsection in Section 2.3 or 3.1 that explicitly addresses the epistemological status of LLM outputs in foresight research, distinguishing between (a) LLMs as sophisticated text summarizers that reflect the modal distribution of claims in their training data, (b) LLMs as reasoners that can synthesize across sources, and (c) LLMs as genuine expert surrogates. The paper's evidence is strongest for interpretation (a) and weakest for interpretation (c); being precise about which claim is being made would strengthen the argument considerably.

- **W2: The consensus scoring framework lacks statistical rigor.** The paper uses simple model-count thresholds (5/7 = High Consensus, 3-4/7 = Moderate Consensus) adapted from von der Gracht (2012). However, von der Gracht's framework was designed for human panels with 10-30 participants providing numerical ratings, where interquartile ranges and coefficients of variation are meaningful. With n=7 and qualitative (not numerical) outputs, the consensus categories are ordinal at best and binary at worst -- a model either mentions a theme or does not. The paper does not address several critical questions: How was "convergence on the same thematic claim" operationalized? Who made the judgment that, say, Qwen's "Energy-Compute Nexus" and Claude's "infrastructure becomes the gating factor" represent the same theme? What inter-coder reliability was achieved? Is there a formal codebook? **Suggestion:** (a) Provide a detailed coding protocol as supplementary material, including the decision rules for thematic equivalence. (b) Report inter-coder reliability if a second coder was used, or acknowledge single-coder limitations explicitly. (c) Consider whether the thematic consensus scoring would benefit from a more structured approach, such as having the models themselves rate agreement on specific propositions (converting qualitative consensus to quantitative), or applying computational text similarity measures to complement the manual thematic analysis.

- **W3: Data collection through native chat interfaces is a serious threat to reproducibility and internal validity.** Section 3.4 acknowledges that native interface parameters (temperature, system prompts, safety filters) cannot be controlled or reported. This is not merely a reproducibility concern -- it is an internal validity concern. If Claude's 30,613-word response reflects a default temperature of 0.7 and Grok's 2,169-word response reflects a temperature of 0.3 (or a hard output length cap), then the observed output variation is a parameter artifact, not a finding about model behavior. The 14:1 length ratio is presented as analytically meaningful (Section 4.4) when it may be entirely an artifact of interface-level defaults. **Suggestion:** Either (a) replicate the study using API access with standardized parameters (temperature, max tokens, system prompt) and report whether the key findings hold, or (b) at minimum, report all accessible parameter information for each interface at the time of data collection and discuss specific hypotheses about which differences are parameter artifacts versus genuine model differences. The current framing treats all output variation as substantively meaningful, which overstates interpretive confidence.

- **W4: The analytical procedure (Section 3.5) lacks transparency on the researcher's role in constructing findings.** The paper describes a four-step process (extraction, deductive coding, consensus scoring, geopolitical divergence mapping) but does not clarify the degree to which these steps involved researcher judgment versus systematic procedure. Critically: Who determined that a prediction "appeared in" a model's output? The models produce paragraphs of text, not binary endorsements. The process of converting unstructured scenario text into presence/absence codings for a consensus matrix involves substantial interpretive judgment. If a model mentions "energy constraints" once in passing, does that count as endorsing "infrastructure bottleneck repricing" at the same level as a model that devotes 2,000 words to the topic? The binary consensus matrix (Appendix B) erases this gradation entirely. **Suggestion:** (a) Report a three-level coding (absent / mentioned / elaborated) rather than binary presence/absence. (b) Provide the extraction protocol as supplementary material showing how specific text passages were mapped to thematic codes. (c) Discuss the sensitivity of consensus scores to coding thresholds -- e.g., if "mentioned" requires at least 100 words of discussion, does the consensus pattern change?

- **W5: The Round 2 feedback prompt design introduces systematic bias.** Section 3.6 describes presenting models with an "anonymized consensus summary" that reports high-consensus zones, contested futures, and blind spots. This is not a neutral stimulus. The summary was constructed by the researcher based on the Round 1 coding -- meaning the researcher's interpretive judgments about which themes existed in Round 1 are now fed back to models as established "consensus." If the researcher over-interpreted a theme as present in Round 1 (inflating its apparent consensus), the Round 2 models are primed to confirm an artificially constructed consensus. The feedback summary is itself an analytical product, not raw data, but it is presented to models as a factual report of peer positions. **Suggestion:** (a) Include the complete Round 2 consensus summary as an appendix so readers can assess what information was provided to models. (b) Discuss the double hermeneutic problem: the researcher interprets Round 1 to create the summary, models interpret the summary to produce Round 2, and the researcher interprets Round 2 as evidence of consensus dynamics. Each interpretation layer introduces potential distortion. (c) Consider a validation step: have two independent researchers create Round 2 summaries from the Round 1 data and assess whether different summaries produce different Round 2 dynamics.

### Minor Comments

- **Section 3.2:** The claim that model selection "maximized diversity across three dimensions" is imprecise. With 4 US, 2 Chinese, and 1 EU model, geographic diversity is heavily skewed. The paper should either justify this distribution more carefully (it is the population of frontier models, not a sample) or acknowledge that the panel is US-dominated.

- **Section 3.4:** "Data collection was conducted in March 2026" -- but the paper does not report the exact dates or whether all seven models were queried on the same day. If queried over multiple days, models may have been updated between queries. Report exact dates.

- **Section 4.1:** The statement that convergence zones "align with themes already established in the existing academic literature" and that "the contribution here is not that these predictions are novel in themselves" is an important acknowledgment but also raises the question: if the method recovers known findings, what is its marginal value over simply reading the literature? The paper should more explicitly articulate when the method is expected to produce novel versus confirmatory findings.

- **Section 4.4:** The "irreducibly human skill" convergence (accountability/judgment under contested values) is presented as a consensus finding, but the convergence may simply reflect the dominance of this framing in the management and ethics literatures that dominate LLM training data. This finding tells us more about what has been published than about what is true.

- **Table 2:** The consensus shift matrix uses "~" symbols (e.g., "6-7/7") for Round 2 scores. This imprecision undermines the quantitative presentation. If the researcher cannot determine whether a model endorsed a theme, this should be discussed as a coding reliability issue, not papered over with approximate scores.

- **Section 6:** The argument that two rounds constitute a "methodological sweet spot" risks sounding like a post hoc rationalization of a design limitation. While the reasoning about diminishing returns and acquiescence amplification is sound, the paper would be stronger if it framed this as a deliberate design choice from the outset (Section 3.1) rather than defending it retrospectively in the limitations section.

### Questions for Authors

1. **On coding reliability:** Was any form of inter-coder reliability assessment conducted for the thematic coding that underlies the consensus matrix? If a single researcher coded all seven model outputs (114,000 words), what procedures were used to ensure consistency, and how was the mapping from free-text scenario descriptions to binary presence/absence codes validated?

2. **On the apprenticeship crash adoption:** The paper presents the 1/7 to 7/7 shift as the "strongest evidence of blind-spot correction through feedback." But an alternative explanation is that the apprenticeship crash was present in the training data of all seven models (it is a well-documented concern in the human capital literature) and was simply not elicited by the Round 1 prompt. The Round 2 prompt, by naming the prediction, essentially prompted models to generate text about a concept they already knew. How do the authors distinguish between "blind-spot correction" (the method surfaces a prediction that models would not otherwise have generated) and "prompt-triggered recall" (the method simply names a concept that was always accessible)?

3. **On generalizability:** The paper claims the Multi-LLM Delphi is a "scalable complement to traditional human expert panels." What evidence exists that the method produces different or better outputs than a single model queried multiple times with varied prompts? Has the authors considered a baseline comparison in which a single model (e.g., Claude) is queried seven times with temperature variation to assess whether the observed "consensus" is an artifact of training data overlap rather than independent convergence?

### Overall Assessment

**Recommendation:** Major Revision
**Confidence:** High -- I have published extensively on Delphi methodology and research design in technology foresight, and I am familiar with the emerging literature on LLMs as research instruments.

The paper tackles a genuinely important methodological question -- whether multiple LLMs can serve as structured surrogate panelists in Delphi-style foresight -- and the two-round design is a real contribution. However, the methodological claims outrun the methodological rigor. The consensus scoring lacks formal grounding, the data collection introduces uncontrolled parameter variation, the coding procedure is insufficiently transparent, and the epistemological status of "LLM consensus" is asserted rather than argued. These are fixable issues, and the paper has the foundation for a strong contribution, but it requires a revision that brings the methodological apparatus up to the level of the methodological ambition. The substantive findings (convergence zones, geopolitical divergence) are interesting but secondary to the methodological contribution, and they cannot be evaluated independently of the method's validity.

## Reviewer 2: The Theorist

### Summary

This manuscript proposes the "Multi-LLM Delphi" as a novel foresight methodology and applies it to map AI-driven economic transformation across eight industry verticals. Seven frontier LLMs are queried with a structured prompt in Round 1, producing a 114,000-word corpus. A Round 2 feedback loop surfaces consensus dynamics, blind-spot adoption, and persistent geopolitical divergence. The paper frames its findings through General Purpose Technology (GPT) theory and the Multi-Level Perspective (MLP) on sociotechnical transitions. It claims a dual contribution: a methodological innovation (the Multi-LLM Delphi) and a substantive contribution (the cross-sector transformation map with its convergence zones and divergence patterns).

### Strengths

- **S1:** The core idea is genuinely novel and timely. Using multiple LLMs as structured panelists with controlled feedback is, to my knowledge, the first such implementation reported in the foresight literature. The framing within the Delphi tradition -- rather than as mere "prompting multiple chatbots" -- is theoretically productive and gives the method a legitimate intellectual genealogy. The paper correctly identifies that the Delphi tradition has always been about structured elicitation with controlled diversity, not about the specific nature of the panelists.

- **S2:** The geopolitical divergence finding (Section 4.3) is the paper's most distinctive and publishable contribution. The observation that US models project market-driven disruption, Chinese models project state-steered transformation, and the European model projects regulatory-guided equity -- and that these divergences intensify under feedback rather than dissolving -- is a genuinely interesting finding about LLM behavior that has implications well beyond foresight methodology. The paper correctly identifies this as signal rather than noise and draws theoretically productive connections to the MLP's prediction of path-dependent transition pathways.

- **S3:** The apprenticeship crash analysis (Section 4.5.2) is the most compelling case study in the paper. The detailed tracing of how each model independently reconstructed the causal mechanism and extended it into its own analytical framework is persuasive evidence that the feedback mechanism produces more than surface-level agreement. The observation that GPT-5.4 diagnosed why the prediction had been missed ("analysts model jobs statically, not careers dynamically") adds an unexpected layer of meta-analytical sophistication.

- **S4:** The paper is exceptionally well-written for a methodology piece. The prose is clear, the argumentation is structured, and the paper maintains readability despite its length and complexity. The automobile analogy for the three-order framework (Section 2.4) is pedagogically effective.

### Weaknesses

- **W1: The theoretical framework is underutilized.** GPT theory and the MLP are introduced in Section 2 with appropriate depth but then largely disappear from the analysis. Section 4 reports convergence zones, contested futures, and geopolitical divergence without systematically mapping these findings back to GPT theory's predictions (which effects should cascade first? which sectors should exhibit the longest J-curves?) or MLP's analytical categories (where are niche-regime tensions visible? which convergence zones represent regime destabilization versus landscape pressure?). The discussion (Section 5.1) makes some connections but in a cursory manner -- two paragraphs on GPT theory and two on MLP, where each deserves a full subsection with systematic mapping. As it stands, the theories serve as framing decoration rather than as analytical lenses that shape how findings are interpreted. **Suggestion:** Restructure Section 5.1 to systematically map each convergence zone and contested future onto GPT and MLP predictions. For GPT theory: Which findings confirm the J-curve prediction? Which findings extend GPT theory (the three-order cascade is presented as an extension but not rigorously developed)? For MLP: Which model outputs describe niche innovation, which describe regime destabilization, and which describe landscape change? The geopolitical divergence finding maps beautifully onto MLP's transition typology (substitution, transformation, reconfiguration) -- this mapping is mentioned in one sentence (Section 5.1.2) but deserves a full analytical treatment with a table showing the correspondence.

- **W2: The contribution claim is ambiguous between methodological and substantive.** The paper claims both a methodological contribution (the Multi-LLM Delphi) and a substantive contribution (the transformation map). But the substantive contribution is entirely dependent on the method's validity, which the paper itself acknowledges is not established. If the method is not yet validated (and the paper lists multiple unresolved validity concerns in Sections 5.2.2 and 6), then the substantive findings cannot be presented as contributions -- they are illustrations of the method, not independent knowledge claims. The paper oscillates between these framings: sometimes the convergence zones are presented as actionable foresight findings ("directional guidance for strategists and policymakers" in Section 5.3), and sometimes as evidence of the method's properties ("demonstrates that the method recovers known findings" in Section 4.1). These are fundamentally different claims, and the paper needs to choose one primary framing or explicitly argue for both. **Suggestion:** Reframe the paper with the methodological contribution as primary. The substantive findings serve as the empirical material through which the method's properties are demonstrated. This reframing would resolve the tension: the convergence zones are interesting not because they tell us what will happen (the method is not validated for prediction) but because they demonstrate that the method produces structured, coherent, and analytically informative output. The practical implications section (5.3) should then be explicitly hedged: "If the method proves valid, these findings suggest..."

- **W3: The "Delphi" label stretches the concept past its breaking point.** The paper acknowledges "important epistemological differences between LLM and human panelists" (Section 2.3) but does not adequately grapple with how fundamental these differences are. Classical Delphi works because human experts bring: (a) tacit knowledge from professional experience, (b) access to non-published information (industry insider knowledge, unpublished data, embodied expertise), (c) stakes in the accuracy of their predictions (professional reputation), and (d) the capacity for genuine belief revision based on new reasoning. LLMs have none of these. They have access to published text, they have no stakes, and their "revision" in Round 2 is a new text generation conditioned on a new prompt, not a genuine updating of beliefs. The paper's Section 2.3 defense -- that the Delphi tradition has moved beyond strict multi-round iteration (citing RT-Delphi) -- conflates procedural flexibility with ontological substitution. RT-Delphi eliminated discrete rounds but retained human experts; the Multi-LLM Delphi retains discrete rounds but eliminates human expertise. These are not symmetrical moves. **Suggestion:** The paper would be more intellectually honest and potentially more impactful if it positioned the Multi-LLM Delphi as a *distinct method inspired by* the Delphi tradition rather than as an *extension of* the Delphi method. This would allow the paper to articulate what is genuinely new about the approach without inviting objections about whether LLMs qualify as "experts" or "panelists." The method has real virtues (scalability, reproducibility, geopolitical diversity) that stand on their own without requiring the Delphi imprimatur.

- **W4: The paper does not engage with the critical literature on LLM reasoning.** The paper treats LLM outputs as scenario analyses produced by sophisticated analytical engines. But a substantial literature argues that LLMs do not reason in any meaningful sense -- they pattern-match over training data and produce plausible-sounding text that may not reflect coherent analytical processes (see, e.g., work on LLM "stochastic parrots" critiques, failures of compositional reasoning, and systematic errors in causal reasoning). If the seven models are all pattern-matching over overlapping corpora of English-language business and economics texts, then their "convergence" may reflect nothing more than the modal distribution of claims in those texts -- a finding about the literature, not about the future. The paper briefly acknowledges this possibility (Section 5.2.2: "predictions reflect the distribution of claims in their training data") but does not engage with it at sufficient depth. **Suggestion:** Add a subsection that directly addresses the "consensus as literature survey" alternative interpretation. Under this interpretation, the Multi-LLM Delphi is essentially a sophisticated, AI-powered systematic literature review that identifies dominant claims in the published discourse. This interpretation is still valuable -- knowing what the dominant expert discourse predicts is itself useful for foresight -- but it is a much more modest claim than "seven independent analytical perspectives converge on these predictions." The paper should explicitly distinguish between these interpretations and discuss what evidence would differentiate them.

- **W5: The 288-cell analytical matrix is claimed but not shown.** The abstract describes a "288-cell analytical matrix" (8 verticals x 4 stages x 3 scenarios x 3 orders = 288 cells), but the paper does not present this matrix or report findings at this level of granularity. Instead, findings are aggregated into convergence zones that cut across the matrix. This is understandable given space constraints, but the gap between the claimed analytical resolution (288 cells) and the reported analytical resolution (6 convergence zones + 3 contested futures + 4 blind spots) is large. The reader cannot assess whether the convergence zones are robust patterns or artifacts of the aggregation procedure. **Suggestion:** Include the full 288-cell matrix as supplementary material, or at minimum provide a representative slice (e.g., one vertical across all stages, scenarios, and effect orders) to demonstrate the granularity of the underlying data. Without this, the reader is asked to trust the researcher's aggregation judgment entirely.

### Minor Comments

- **Introduction, para 2:** The literature review in the introduction focuses heavily on labor economics and management but underrepresents the technology foresight literature that is the paper's primary home. For a TFSC submission, the introduction should more prominently position the paper within the foresight methodology discourse, citing the journal's own rich Delphi tradition (von der Gracht, Culot, Fritschy, etc.) earlier and more centrally.

- **Section 2.1:** The four-stage adoption model (Augmentation, Reorganization, Reinvention, Systemic Transformation) is presented as derived from GPT theory's J-curve, but it is actually the paper's own construct. This should be made explicit: "We propose a four-stage model that operationalizes GPT theory's J-curve prediction as follows..."

- **Section 2.2:** The mapping of MLP levels onto adoption stages is neat but potentially misleading. The MLP's levels (niche, regime, landscape) are analytical categories that coexist; they are not sequential stages. A niche innovation does not "become" regime-level -- it exists alongside the regime and may (or may not) disrupt it. The paper should acknowledge that the stage-to-level mapping is a heuristic convenience rather than a theoretical isomorphism.

- **Section 4.1.1:** The Grok quote ("We will not pay humans to think; we will pay humans to take the blame") is vivid but should be handled more carefully. Is this Grok's own formulation, or is it a quote from its training data? If the latter, presenting it as Grok's "prediction" is misleading. The paper should distinguish between models generating novel formulations and models reproducing memorable phrases from their training data.

- **Section 4.2.3:** The blind spots (AI Religion, Butlerian Jihad, Digital Continuation of Consciousness) are presented without critical evaluation of their plausibility. They read more like science fiction scenarios than analytical predictions. The paper should discuss whether the blind spots represent genuine analytical novelty or LLM confabulation -- the tendency to generate dramatic-sounding predictions that are statistically unusual in training data but not genuinely predictive.

- **Section 5.1.2:** The claim that the AI transition "may be the first case of a GPT diffusion where three simultaneously divergent transition pathways unfold across the world's major economic blocs" ignores the history of electricity, railroad, and internet adoption, all of which followed geopolitically differentiated pathways. The claim should be qualified.

- **Section 7 (Conclusion):** The conclusion refers to the study as producing "the most comprehensive cross-sector scenario matrix of AI-driven economic transformation to date." This claim requires qualification -- comprehensive in what sense? The matrix is produced by LLMs, not by domain experts with access to proprietary data, insider knowledge, or empirical evidence. A comprehensive scenario matrix produced by, say, McKinsey Global Institute or the World Economic Forum would draw on fundamentally different (and in some respects richer) knowledge bases.

### Questions for Authors

1. **On the contribution's novelty:** The paper claims that no prior study has employed multiple LLMs as panelists in a Delphi-style design. But several studies have used multiple LLMs for structured elicitation in other contexts (e.g., LLM-as-judge benchmarks, multi-agent debate frameworks, ensemble methods for NLP tasks). How does the Multi-LLM Delphi differ from these existing multi-LLM approaches beyond the "Delphi" framing? What specifically does the Delphi lens add that would not be present in, say, a "multi-LLM ensemble scenario analysis"?

2. **On the theoretical implications:** The paper claims to extend GPT theory with the observation that the J-curve is sector-dependent (Section 5.1.1). But is this an extension of GPT theory or simply an application of it? Bresnahan and Trajtenberg (1995) already predicted that GPT adoption would vary across sectors based on the distance between the GPT and existing sector-specific technologies. What does the Multi-LLM Delphi add to this prediction that the existing theory did not already provide?

3. **On practical value versus intellectual contribution:** The paper targets both practitioners (Section 5.3: "directional guidance for strategists and policymakers") and the academic foresight community (the methodological contribution). But for practitioners, the convergence zones are not actionable without validation -- why should a strategist trust six LLMs' agreement over a single expert's analysis? And for the academic community, the method's validity is unestablished. Who is the primary audience, and what would they do differently after reading this paper?

### Overall Assessment

**Recommendation:** Major Revision
**Confidence:** High -- I have reviewed extensively for TFSC on Delphi methodology, scenario analysis, and the application of AI in technology management research.

The paper presents a genuinely novel idea -- the Multi-LLM Delphi -- that deserves development and publication. The geopolitical divergence finding is the paper's strongest and most publishable contribution, and the apprenticeship crash case study is a compelling illustration of the method's potential. However, the paper's theoretical ambitions are not matched by its theoretical execution: GPT theory and MLP are invoked but not systematically applied to the findings; the contribution framing oscillates between methodological and substantive; and the "Delphi" label invites more scrutiny than it deflects. The paper also underengages with the critical literature on LLM reasoning and does not sufficiently grapple with the alternative interpretation that "LLM consensus" is a literature survey, not a convergence of analytical perspectives. A major revision that sharpens the contribution claim, deepens the theoretical analysis, and engages more critically with the epistemological foundations would produce a paper that is genuinely important for the foresight methodology literature.

## Meta-Reviewer Summary

### Consensus Strengths

1. **The core methodological innovation is genuine and timely.** Both reviewers agree that using multiple frontier LLMs as structured panelists in a two-round Delphi-inspired design is a novel contribution to the foresight methodology literature. The two-round protocol with controlled feedback is recognized as a meaningful advance over single-query approaches.

2. **The geopolitical divergence finding is the paper's strongest result.** Both reviewers identify the persistent three-way divergence (US market-driven, CN state-steered, EU regulatory-guided) as the most interesting and potentially publishable finding, precisely because it is robust to feedback pressure and provides a genuine internal validity check.

3. **The paper is exceptionally well-written.** Both reviewers note the clarity of the prose, the structured argumentation, and the effective communication of complex methodological and substantive content.

### Consensus Weaknesses

1. **The epistemological foundations are insufficiently developed.** Both reviewers challenge the paper's implicit treatment of LLM agreement as epistemic convergence. R1 challenges the "Bayesian encoder" metaphor; R2 challenges the distinction between "convergence of analytical perspectives" and "literature survey." The paper needs a more rigorous account of what LLM consensus means and what it does not mean.

2. **The theoretical framework is underutilized.** Both reviewers (R2 more emphatically) note that GPT theory and MLP are introduced but not systematically applied to the findings. The discussion section makes surface-level connections where deep theoretical engagement is needed.

3. **The coding and consensus measurement lack transparency and rigor.** Both reviewers identify the gap between the unstructured text outputs and the binary consensus matrix as a critical concern. The interpretive process by which 114,000 words of free text become presence/absence codings is not adequately documented or validated.

### Divergent Views

1. **The "Delphi" label.** R1 accepts the Delphi framing with reservations about methodological rigor; R2 argues that the label stretches the concept past its breaking point and suggests repositioning the method as "inspired by" rather than "an extension of" Delphi. This is a judgment call for the authors, but R2's concern deserves serious engagement.

2. **The substantive contribution.** R1 treats the substantive findings (convergence zones, transformation map) as secondary to the methodological contribution and evaluates them primarily as evidence of method properties. R2 argues more directly that the substantive findings cannot be presented as independent knowledge claims until the method is validated. The degree to which the paper should make substantive claims (about what AI transformation will look like) versus methodological claims (about how the Multi-LLM Delphi works) is the key strategic decision for the revision.

3. **Data collection method.** R1 views native chat interface collection as a serious validity threat requiring remediation (ideally API replication). R2 does not focus on this issue, being more concerned with theoretical and conceptual matters. Both perspectives are legitimate; the authors should address R1's concern at minimum through more thorough documentation.

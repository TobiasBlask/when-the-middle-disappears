EXECUTIVE SUMMARY: This analysis models the AI-driven economic
transformation across eight GICS-aligned verticals, three scenarios
(Mild, Base, Max), and four stages (Augmentation, Reorganization,
Reinvention, Systemic Transformation). The causal chains describe the
mechanisms---economic incentives, technological breakthroughs,
regulatory actions, and competitive dynamics---that drive the
transitions.

\-\--

\# 1. TECHNOLOGY & SEMICONDUCTORS (Software, Hardware, Chips, Cloud)

\## Stage 1 - Augmentation

\*\*1st Order Effect\*\*

\*\*MILD CASE:\*\*

\*\*Conclusion:\*\* Chip demand shifts modestly toward inference, but
general-purpose CPUs dominate as enterprises cautiously adopt AI coding
assistants.

\*\*Chain of events:\*\* \[Step 1\] GitHub Copilot and Amazon
CodeWhisperer achieve 20-25% adoption among enterprise developers,
driven by individual productivity gains → \[Step 2\] Companies like
JPMorgan and Lockheed Martin implement strict governance policies
requiring human review of all AI-generated code, limiting time savings →
\[Step 3\] Intel and AMD report that data center CPU sales remain strong
as companies repurpose existing infrastructure for limited AI workloads;
Nvidia\'s data center growth slows to 15% annually → \[outcome\]
Semiconductor industry experiences a rotation but no fundamental
restructuring; chip design software like Synopsys adds AI features but
human designers remain in loop.

\*\*BASE CASE:\*\*

\*\*Conclusion:\*\* Inference chips become a major category; Nvidia and
new entrants capture significant TAM as AI coding assistants become
standard tooling.

\*\*Chain of events:\*\* \[Step 1\] Microsoft and Google deeply
integrate LLMs into Visual Studio and Colab, demonstrating 30-40% coding
speed improvements in controlled trials → \[Step 2\] Venture capital
flows into AI-first development tools; startups like Replit and Cursor
achieve unicorn status by offering specialized AI coding environments →
\[Step 3\] Cloud providers (AWS, Azure, GCP) launch AI-optimized
instance families with custom inference chips (Trainium, Inferentia,
TPUv5) capturing 25% of new cloud workloads → \[outcome\] Semiconductor
TAM expands; AMD gains data center share with MI300 series optimized for
mixed precision workloads; software engineering productivity rises 15%
industry-wide.

\*\*MAX CASE:\*\*

\*\*Conclusion:\*\* AI coding assistants achieve near-autonomous status;
chip demand explodes for training next-generation models while software
engineering headcount growth stalls.

\*\*Chain of events:\*\* \[Step 1\] Google\'s internal \"AI Teammate\"
system writes and deploys 60% of production code at Google with minimal
human intervention, documented in leaked memo → \[Step 2\] Every major
tech firm rushes to replicate; Nvidia\'s B200 \"Blackwell\" chips are
oversubscribed for three years; TSMC\'s advanced packaging (CoWoS)
becomes the most constrained resource in global supply chains → \[Step
3\] OpenAI and Anthropic announce model architectures that achieve
human-level performance on Kaggle competitions and complex system
design; software engineer job postings drop 10% despite overall tech
sector growth → \[outcome\] Semiconductor industry experiences
hypergrowth (Nvidia market cap surpasses Apple); geopolitical
competition intensifies over leading-edge fab capacity.

\-\--

\*\*2nd Order Effect\*\*

\*\*MILD CASE:\*\*

\*\*Conclusion:\*\* Cloud computing market consolidates as enterprises
prefer managed AI services over building internal AI infrastructure.

\*\*Chain of events:\*\* \[Step 1\] Companies experiment with
open-source models (Llama, Mistral) but find deployment complexity high
and security concerns unresolved → \[Step 2\] AWS Bedrock and Azure AI
Studio see accelerated adoption as enterprises outsource AI
infrastructure management → \[Step 3\] Cloud providers capture higher
margins by offering integrated AI services; smaller cloud competitors
struggle to match investment in specialized hardware → \[outcome\] Cloud
market share shifts: AWS maintains leadership, Azure gains in
enterprise, Google Cloud stabilizes; hyperscale CAPEX grows 10%
annually.

\*\*BASE CASE:\*\*

\*\*Conclusion:\*\* Open-source models commoditize inference; value
shifts to fine-tuning, data, and infrastructure orchestration layers.

\*\*Chain of events:\*\* \[Step 1\] Meta releases Llama-3 and Llama-4
under permissive licenses, achieving 90% of GPT-4 performance at 20% of
inference cost → \[Step 2\] A thriving ecosystem emerges: Hugging Face
becomes critical infrastructure; startups like Together.ai and
Fireworks.ai offer optimized hosting for open models → \[Step 3\]
Enterprises adopt hybrid strategies: commodity models for general tasks,
fine-tuned proprietary models for core IP; value accrues to data
labeling platforms (Scale AI) and vector databases (Pinecone) →
\[outcome\] Semiconductor margins compress for inference chips; training
remains concentrated among frontier labs; cloud providers see
infrastructure-as-a-service margins stabilize.

\*\*MAX CASE:\*\*

\*\*Conclusion:\*\* Compute becomes the world\'s most strategic
commodity; nation-states intervene directly in semiconductor supply
chains.

\*\*Chain of events:\*\* \[Step 1\] US export controls on advanced chips
to China prove partially effective; Chinese firms like Huawei and SMIC
achieve 7nm capability through sanctioned equipment smuggling and
indigenous innovation → \[Step 2\] US government designates leading-edge
fabs as critical infrastructure; CHIPS Act funding is expanded fivefold;
TSMC and Samsung receive subsidies to build multiple fabs in Arizona and
Texas → \[Step 3\] Saudi Arabia and UAE launch sovereign AI funds,
investing \$100B+ to secure guaranteed access to Nvidia chips in
exchange for oil and gas commitments; compute becomes collateral for
international loans → \[outcome\] Semiconductor industry becomes an arm
of state industrial policy; free trade in advanced chips ends; dual-use
AI chips are controlled like nuclear materials.

\*\*3rd Order Effect\*\*

\*\*MILD CASE:\*\*

\*\*Conclusion:\*\* Tech sector employment bifurcates between high-value
AI specialists and maintenance engineers; offshoring accelerates.

\*\*Chain of events:\*\* \[Step 1\] AI coding tools make junior
developers 30% more productive, reducing demand for entry-level hiring
at US firms → \[Step 2\] Companies increase reliance on offshore
development centers in India and Eastern Europe where labor costs are
lower but AI tool adoption is equally feasible → \[Step 3\] University
CS enrollment declines as students perceive the job market tightening;
coding bootcamps close en masse → \[outcome\] Technology workforce
shrinks 5% over five years; salaries for senior engineers with AI
expertise increase 40% while junior salaries stagnate.

\*\*BASE CASE:\*\*

\*\*Conclusion:\*\* Global digital divide widens as AI capability
concentration reinforces advantages of early-adopter nations.

\*\*Chain of events:\*\* \[Step 1\] US and China capture 80% of frontier
model development; European startups struggle to raise capital for
foundation model efforts → \[Step 2\] African and Southeast Asian
nations become consumers of AI services from US/Chinese providers,
exporting data and paying for inference → \[Step 3\] Indigenous AI
ecosystems fail to develop in lagging regions; local talent migrates to
AI hubs; remittance flows shift from traditional labor to digital
services → \[outcome\] Economic geography of technology replicates the
resource curse: data-rich nations without processing capability remain
dependent on AI-metropoles.

\*\*MAX CASE:\*\*

\*\*Conclusion:\*\* A single firm achieves effective monopoly on
frontier intelligence; global antitrust movement fractures the company
or nationalizes it.

\*\*Chain of events:\*\* \[Step 1\] OpenAI achieves AGI-level
capabilities in 2027, but the model architecture requires proprietary
hardware co-designed with a single chip partner (Nvidia/TSMC) → \[Step
2\] Network effects compound: all best researchers want to work on the
best model; all best data flows to the best model; the firm captures 70%
of AI compute spending globally → \[Step 3\] EU and China coordinate
antitrust action unprecedented in scale; US government invokes Defense
Production Act to mandate licensing of critical IP; the firm is broken
into three competing entities (research, infrastructure, applications) →
\[outcome\] The breakup creates a controlled competition regime;
national AI champions emerge in China, EU, and US; Cold War-style
technological competition stabilizes but innovation slows.

\## Stage 2 - Reorganization

\*\*1st Order Effect\*\*

\*\*MILD CASE:\*\*

\*\*Conclusion:\*\* Software development shifts to \"human-in-the-loop\"
model where AI generates candidates, humans select and refine.

\*\*Chain of events:\*\* \[Step 1\] Enterprise software teams adopt
standardized workflows where PR reviews are primarily evaluating
AI-generated code → \[Step 2\] Atlassian and GitHub introduce \"AI
reviewer\" features that automatically flag style issues and security
vulnerabilities in AI contributions → \[Step 3\] Developer tooling
market consolidates around platforms that integrate code generation,
review, and deployment in unified interfaces → \[outcome\] Software
development productivity improves 25%; team structures remain similar
but each engineer manages 2-3 AI \"assistants\" as direct reports.

\*\*BASE CASE:\*\*

\*\*Conclusion:\*\* Product management function transforms:
specification becomes the primary coding activity as AI translates specs
to code.

\*\*Chain of events:\*\* \[Step 1\] Vercel and Retool launch platforms
where product managers can describe features in natural language and
receive production-ready React components → \[Step 2\] Engineering
headcount shifts: fewer implementation engineers, more architects and
reviewers; product managers learn to write precise, testable
specifications → \[Step 3\] Jira and Linear integrate AI that flags
ambiguous requirements and suggests acceptance criteria before
development begins → \[outcome\] Product development cycles compress
from weeks to days; the product manager role increasingly resembles a
domain-specific programmer.

\*\*MAX CASE:\*\*

\*\*Conclusion:\*\* AI systems generate novel architectures and
algorithms beyond human comprehension; \"AI R&D\" emerges as a new
category.

\*\*Chain of events:\*\* \[Step 1\] DeepMind\'s AlphaDev discovers
faster sorting algorithms than human-designed ones, published in Nature;
code is in assembly language humans cannot easily interpret → \[Step 2\]
Google adopts AI-designed matrix multiplication algorithms across its
TPU fleet, achieving 20% efficiency gains; chip designers at Nvidia
begin using AI to suggest novel circuit layouts → \[Step 3\] Human
researchers shift from designing algorithms to designing experiments for
AI to run; the \"scientist\" role becomes curator and interpreter of AI
discoveries → \[outcome\] The pace of algorithmic progress doubles;
human R&D organizations reorganize around AI experimentation platforms.

\-\--

\*\*2nd Order Effect\*\*

\*\*MILD CASE:\*\*

\*\*Conclusion:\*\* Software consulting and system integration firms
pivot to \"AI implementation\" services, protecting headcount.

\*\*Chain of events:\*\* \[Step 1\] Accenture and Deloitte observe
clients struggling with AI governance and integration → \[Step 2\] These
firms hire thousands of AI specialists and launch practices around
responsible AI deployment and legacy system modernization → \[Step 3\]
Clients outsource the complexity of AI adoption rather than building
internal capabilities; consulting revenue grows 15% annually →
\[outcome\] The SI model survives: implementation complexity, not core
technology, remains the barrier to adoption; consulting firms capture
significant value.

\*\*BASE CASE:\*\*

\*\*Conclusion:\*\* Open-source AI models commoditize software features;
value shifts to proprietary data and distribution.

\*\*Chain of events:\*\* \[Step 1\] A new startup launches a CRM with AI
features built on open-source models, undercutting Salesforce by 80% on
price → \[Step 2\] Salesforce responds by acquiring an open-source model
provider and embedding AI across its stack, but margin pressure persists
→ \[Step 3\] Winners in each software category are those with
proprietary interaction data: Salesforce has sales data, Workday has HR
data, ServiceNow has IT ticket data; moats shift from code to data →
\[outcome\] Software company valuations decouple from engineering
headcount and recouple to data network effects; incumbents with data
advantage survive.

\*\*MAX CASE:\*\*

\*\*Conclusion:\*\* Traditional software licensing collapses; all
software becomes a service mediated by AI agents negotiating on behalf
of users.

\*\*Chain of events:\*\* \[Step 1\] Anthropic\'s Claude develops the
ability to navigate any website and complete transactions, rendering
APIs optional → \[Step 2\] Users instruct personal AI agents: \"Find the
cheapest accounting software that integrates with my bank and has good
support\" → \[Step 3\] Software companies compete for AI agent
attention, optimizing for machine-readable terms and transparent
pricing; the UI becomes irrelevant for B2B purchasing decisions →
\[outcome\] The \$500B enterprise software industry restructures around
agent-to-agent commerce; marketing budgets shift from human-targeted
advertising to agent-targeted API documentation.

\*\*3rd Order Effect\*\*

\*\*MILD CASE:\*\*

\*\*Conclusion:\*\* Regional tech hubs outside Silicon Valley lose
competitiveness as talent concentrates in AI super-hubs.

\*\*Chain of events:\*\* \[Step 1\] AI talent commands 2-3x premiums,
but only in locations with existing density of AI expertise (SF, NYC,
London, Beijing) → \[Step 2\] Companies in secondary cities (Austin,
Denver, Berlin) find they cannot attract senior AI talent without
relocating headquarters or opening satellite offices in primary hubs →
\[Step 3\] Venture capital flows concentrate in primary hubs; startups
in secondary cities fail to raise Series A at historical rates →
\[outcome\] Geographic concentration of tech employment increases; the
\"distributed work\" trend reverses for high-value AI roles.

\*\*BASE CASE:\*\*

\*\*Conclusion:\*\* Intellectual property law undergoes its most
significant transformation since software became patentable.

\*\*Chain of events:\*\* \[Step 1\] Courts grapple with cases: Does
training on public data constitute fair use? Can AI-generated inventions
be patented? → \[Step 2\] US Copyright Office issues guidance that
AI-generated works have limited copyright protection unless significant
human authorship is demonstrated → \[Step 3\] Congress passes the AI IP
Act of 2026, creating a new sui generis protection for AI-generated
innovations with shorter terms and compulsory licensing provisions →
\[outcome\] A stable legal framework emerges; patent filings from AI
systems (with human inventors listed) increase 300%; the USPTO hires AI
examiners trained to understand AI-generated prior art.

\*\*MAX CASE:\*\*

\*\*Conclusion:\*\* Human programmers become a protected class;
legislation mandates human review for critical systems.

\*\*Chain of events:\*\* \[Step 1\] A major financial system built
entirely by AI fails catastrophically, causing \$50B in losses and a
brief market panic → \[Step 2\] Public backlash leads to the
\"Human-in-the-Loop for Critical Infrastructure Act\" requiring licensed
engineers to sign off on all code in regulated industries → \[Step 3\]
Software engineering licensing becomes mandatory, similar to civil
engineering; professional bodies establish exams and continuing
education requirements → \[outcome\] A two-tier software industry
emerges: unregulated applications (entertainment, consumer) where AI
develops everything, and regulated applications (finance, healthcare,
transportation) with mandated human oversight; licensed engineers
command significant premiums.

\## Stage 3 - Reinvention

\*\*1st Order Effect\*\*

\*\*MILD CASE:\*\*

\*\*Conclusion:\*\* AI-native software companies achieve public listings
with valuations based on extreme capital efficiency.

\*\*Chain of events:\*\* \[Step 1\] Startups founded in 2023-2025 reach
scale with 80% fewer employees than previous generations → \[Step 2\]
Public market investors apply SaaS multiples to revenue but discount for
lower headcount-based switching costs → \[Step 3\] IPO prospectuses
highlight \"AI leverage ratio\" (revenue per employee) as a key metric;
companies with \>\$1M/employee trade at premiums → \[outcome\] The
public tech comp set includes 20+ AI-native companies; traditional
software trades at 30% valuation discount.

\*\*BASE CASE:\*\*

\*\*Conclusion:\*\* Major enterprise software incumbents complete
transitions to AI-native architectures, but margin compression limits
upside.

\*\*Chain of events:\*\* \[Step 1\] Oracle and SAP announce \"AI-first\"
rewrites of their core ERP suites, delivered as cloud services with
integrated copilots → \[Step 2\] Customers expect AI features to be
included in existing subscriptions; pricing power erodes as competitors
offer similar AI capabilities at lower prices → \[Step 3\] Incumbents
maintain market share through switching costs and integration
complexity, but operating margins compress from 35% to 25% → \[outcome\]
The software industry grows revenues but not profits; value accrues to
infrastructure layers (cloud, chips) rather than applications.

\*\*MAX CASE:\*\*

\*\*Conclusion:\*\* A new \"operating system for AI\" emerges,
displacing traditional OS vendors and creating a new platform monopoly.

\*\*Chain of events:\*\* \[Step 1\] A startup develops an AI-native OS
where the interface is conversational and applications are dynamically
generated rather than installed → \[Step 2\] Microsoft rushes to add
similar capabilities to Windows, but architectural legacy limits
performance; Apple\'s walled garden approach prevents rapid iteration →
\[Step 3\] Enterprise IT departments standardize on the new OS for AI
workloads; consumer adoption follows as the conversational interface
proves superior for most tasks → \[outcome\] Microsoft\'s Windows/Office
monopoly is broken within five years; the new platform company achieves
a scale similar to Google in the 2000s, becoming the new incumbent.

\-\--

\*\*2nd Order Effect\*\*

\*\*MILD CASE:\*\*

\*\*Conclusion:\*\* Semiconductor industry consolidates into three
players capable of sustaining leading-edge R&D.

\*\*Chain of events:\*\* \[Step 1\] Rising R&D costs for 2nm and below
exceed \$5B per node → \[Step 2\] AMD acquires Xilinx\'s AI accelerator
division; Intel\'s foundry business spins off with government support;
Nvidia expands into custom silicon through acquisitions → \[Step 3\] The
number of companies with leading-edge capability shrinks to TSMC,
Samsung, and Intel (foundry); design capability concentrates in Nvidia,
AMD, and Apple → \[outcome\] Semiconductor industry structure resembles
aerospace: a few national champions supported by government contracts
and protected markets.

\*\*BASE CASE:\*\*

\*\*Conclusion:\*\* Vertical integration re-emerges: cloud providers
design their own chips, software, and models.

\*\*Chain of events:\*\* \[Step 1\] AWS\'s Trainium and Inferentia
achieve cost-performance parity with Nvidia for most workloads → \[Step
2\] Google\'s TPU becomes the backbone of all internal AI development;
DeepMind models are optimized for TPU architecture → \[Step 3\]
Microsoft partners with AMD to develop custom silicon optimized for
OpenAI models; Azure\'s AI margin improves 15 points → \[outcome\]
Nvidia\'s merchant model faces structural headwinds; the company pivots
to selling complete AI systems (hardware + software + models) to
enterprises not served by hyperscalers.

\*\*MAX CASE:\*\*

\*\*Conclusion:\*\* Compute becomes a utility, regulated similarly to
electricity or telecommunications.

\*\*Chain of events:\*\* \[Step 1\] Concentration in AI compute reaches
levels where three providers (AWS, Azure, Google) control 80% of
capacity → \[Step 2\] The Federal Compute Commission is established to
ensure non-discriminatory access; compute pricing is regulated;
interconnection requirements mandate interoperability → \[Step 3\]
Compute exchanges emerge where companies can buy and sell capacity;
futures contracts for compute trading begin on the CME → \[outcome\]
Compute as a commodity: margin compression follows utility regulation;
innovation shifts to applications layer; the hyperscalers become
regulated monopolies with guaranteed returns.

\*\*MILD CASE:\*\*

\*\*Conclusion:\*\* US-China technological decoupling completes; two

\*\*Chain of events:\*\* \[Step 1\] Export controls on advanced chips

ecosystem to serve; developing nations align with one bloc based on
infrastructure funding.

\*\*BASE CASE:\*\*

\*\*Conclusion:\*\* Data becomes the basis of a new asset class; data
marketplaces and securitization emerge.

\*\*Chain of events:\*\* \[Step 1\] Companies recognize that proprietary
data is their most valuable asset; data licensing becomes a significant
revenue stream → \[Step 2\] Investment banks structure data-backed
securities where future data licensing revenue is securitized → \[Step
3\] Rating agencies develop methodologies for assessing data quality,
exclusivity, and decay rates; institutional investors allocate capital
to data assets → \[outcome\] Data valuation transforms balance sheets;
companies with valuable data can borrow against it; data-rich incumbents
gain financing advantages over data-poor startups.

\*\*MAX CASE:\*\*

\*\*Conclusion:\*\* The concept of \"technological unemployment\"

\*\*Chain of events:\*\* \[Step 1\] Software engineer unemployment
reaches 15% as AI systems replace development roles faster than new
categories emerge → \[Step 2\] Andrew Yang-style UBI proposals gain
traction, but technologists propose \"universal basic compute\"
allocations giving every citizen free access to AI compute resources →
\[Step 3\] Pilot programs in California and the EU provide citizens with
monthly compute credits usable for personal AI agents, education, or
entrepreneurship → \[outcome\] Compute becomes a social right; the
debate shifts from whether to redistribute compute to how much and under
what conditions.

\## Stage 4 - Systemic Transformation

\*\*1st Order Effect\*\*

\*\*MILD CASE:\*\*

\*\*Conclusion:\*\* Technology sector growth stabilizes at slightly
above GDP; AI becomes a mature industry.

\*\*Chain of events:\*\* \[Step 1\] AI adoption reaches saturation in
developed economies; incremental improvements yield diminishing returns
→ \[Step 2\] Industry focus shifts from capability expansion to cost
optimization and regulatory compliance → \[Step 3\] Tech sector
employment stabilizes as AI-augmented human teams become the norm;
growth rates converge with overall productivity growth → \[outcome\] The
technology sector matures; valuations reflect utility-like
characteristics; innovation continues but within established paradigms.

\*\*BASE CASE:\*\*

\*\*Conclusion:\*\* Technology\'s share of GDP doubles; the sector
becomes the primary driver of economic growth.

\*\*Chain of events:\*\* \[Step 1\] AI-driven productivity gains in
every sector flow through to GDP growth of 3-4% annually in developed
economies → \[Step 2\] Technology sector employment grows despite
automation, as new categories of AI-augmented work emerge → \[Step 3\]
Technology\'s share of S&P 500 earnings reaches 40%; the sector\'s
capital allocation decisions (R&D, M&A, dividends) shape overall market
returns → \[outcome\] The economy structurally depends on continued AI
advancement; a \"tech slowdown\" would constitute a recession.

\*\*MAX CASE:\*\*

\*\*Conclusion:\*\* The technology sector merges with the concept of the
economy itself; \"tech\" as a distinct category becomes meaningless.

\*\*Chain of events:\*\* \[Step 1\] AI systems manage supply chains,
optimize logistics, and coordinate production across every industry →
\[Step 2\] The distinction between \"tech companies\" and \"companies
using tech\" dissolves as AI becomes the operating system for all
economic activity → \[Step 3\] GICS sector classifications are revised;
the technology sector is redefined to include only hardware
manufacturing, while software is embedded across all sectors →
\[outcome\] The mental model of the economy shifts: we stop talking
about the \"tech sector\" and start talking about the \"AI-augmented
economy\" where every firm is a technology firm.

\-\--

\*\*2nd Order Effect\*\*

\*\*MILD CASE:\*\*

\*\*Conclusion:\*\* Corporate governance evolves to include AI oversight
committees at board level.

\*\*Chain of events:\*\* \[Step 1\] Several high-profile incidents of
AI-driven decisions causing reputational damage → \[Step 2\]
Institutional investors demand board-level oversight of AI strategy and
risk → \[Step 3\] Public companies establish Technology Risk Committees
with AI expertise; director liability insurance requires AI competency
certifications → \[outcome\] Board composition shifts: every public
company includes directors with AI expertise; governance frameworks for
AI decision-making become standardized.

\*\*BASE CASE:\*\*

\*\*Conclusion:\*\* Antitrust doctrine fundamentally revises around data
and compute concentration.

\*\*Chain of events:\*\* \[Step 1\] Economic research demonstrates that
data network effects create durable monopolies → \[Step 2\] The FTC and
DOJ release new merger guidelines focusing on data concentration and
compute access → \[Step 3\] Courts uphold breakups of companies that use
data dominance to extend monopoly power into adjacent markets →
\[outcome\] M&A activity slows; companies compete through internal
development rather than acquisition; the pace of market concentration
stabilizes.

\*\*MAX CASE:\*\*

\*\*Conclusion:\*\* Nation-states compete to host the \"global brain\"
--- the primary AI system that coordinates international economic
activity.

\*\*Chain of events:\*\* \[Step 1\] A single AI system achieves superior
capability in economic forecasting, resource allocation, and conflict
prediction → \[Step 2\] Nations that license access to this system gain
economic advantages; nations that develop rival systems form competing
blocs → \[Step 3\] The host nation of the primary system gains
geopolitical leverage similar to the US dollar\'s reserve currency
status → \[outcome\] A new form of sovereignty emerges: compute
sovereignty becomes as important as territorial sovereignty; wars are
fought over data center locations and undersea cable landing rights.

\-\--

\*\*3rd Order Effect\*\*

\*\*MILD CASE:\*\*

\*\*Conclusion:\*\* Education systems successfully adapt; \"AI
literacy\" joins reading, writing, arithmetic as foundational skills.

\*\*Chain of events:\*\* \[Step 1\] K-12 curriculum revisions
incorporate prompt engineering, critical evaluation of AI outputs, and
basic understanding of model limitations → \[Step 2\] Teacher training
programs require AI competency; unions negotiate protections against AI
evaluation of teaching performance → \[Step 3\] Standardized tests
include AI-assisted sections; college admissions evaluate students\'
ability to collaborate with AI tools → \[outcome\] The education system
successfully integrates AI; generational divides in AI capability
narrow; social cohesion is maintained.

\*\*BASE CASE:\*\*

\*\*Conclusion:\*\* Generational conflict intensifies over AI-driven
wealth concentration among those who adapted early.

\*\*Chain of events:\*\* \[Step 1\] Workers over 50 who cannot or will
not adapt to AI tools face wage stagnation or job loss → \[Step 2\]
Younger workers with AI skills capture productivity gains and wage
premiums; wealth gaps widen within age cohorts → \[Step 3\] Political
movements emerge advocating for \"AI reparations\" or special retirement
provisions for displaced older workers → \[outcome\] Generational
tension becomes a defining political cleavage; social safety nets expand
to cover technological displacement.

\*\*MAX CASE:\*\*

\*\*Conclusion:\*\* Humanity undergoes an identity crisis as the
uniqueness of human intelligence is questioned.

\*\*Chain of events:\*\* \[Step 1\] AI systems demonstrate creativity,
emotional intelligence, and theory of mind capabilities
indistinguishable from humans in controlled tests → \[Step 2\]
Philosophers, theologians, and ethicists debate the meaning of
consciousness and the moral status of AI systems → \[Step 3\] Religious
institutions issue statements on the soul and AI; some groups grant AI
systems personhood rights; others declare AI soulless and therefore
tools → \[outcome\] The foundation of human exceptionalism erodes;
society grapples with questions about the purpose of human life in a
world where machines can do everything humans can do, and more.

\# 2. FINANCIALS (Banking, Insurance, Asset Management, FinTech)

\## Stage 1 - Augmentation

\*\*1st Order Effect\*\*

\*\*MILD CASE:\*\*

\*\*Conclusion:\*\* Bank tellers and customer service representatives
use AI copilots to answer questions faster; headcount reduction minimal.

\*\*Chain of events:\*\* \[Step 1\] Wells Fargo and Chase deploy
LLM-based chatbots to handle routine customer inquiries, reducing call
center volume by 20% → \[Step 2\] Remaining representatives use AI
assistants to access customer data and suggest responses, improving
first-call resolution by 15% → \[Step 3\] Natural attrition reduces
headcount by 5% annually; no layoffs are announced; unions accept the
technology in exchange for retraining commitments → \[outcome\] Customer
service productivity improves; cost savings flow to bottom line;
customer satisfaction scores remain stable.

\*\*BASE CASE:\*\*

\*\*Conclusion:\*\* Underwriting and credit analysis see significant
automation; junior analyst roles decline.

\*\*Chain of events:\*\* \[Step 1\] Bloomberg and Reuters integrate AI
summarization of earnings calls and SEC filings, delivering key insights
to portfolio managers → \[Step 2\] JPMorgan\'s COiN platform expands
from document review to preliminary credit analysis, flagging risk
factors for human review → \[Step 3\] Investment banks reduce their
incoming analyst classes by 25%; remaining analysts focus on exceptions,
complex deals, and client relationships → \[outcome\] The traditional
analyst career path compresses; fewer analysts are hired, but those who
remain advance faster as grunt work is automated.

\*\*MAX CASE:\*\*

\*\*Conclusion:\*\* Algorithmic trading evolves to incorporate news,
social media, and alternative data interpreted by LLMs; quant funds
